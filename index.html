01 · The Big Idea

It sounds like a
quality technique.

It isn't — not really. The scope of QFD includes quality, but its principal focus is product design.

The objective: design products that will satisfy or exceed customer requirements. Most projects share that goal, but their approach is informal and unsystematic. QFD replaces guesswork with a formal, structured method.

Quality function deployment is a systematic procedure for defining customer desires and requirements and interpreting them in terms of product features, process requirements, and quality characteristics.

— Chapter 23.3

02 · The Cascade

Four matrices,
one translation.

A series of interconnected matrices establish the relationships between customer needs and the technical features of a new product. Each matrix's outputs become the next matrix's inputs.

PHASE 01

Customer Requirements
→ Technical Requirements

PHASE 02

Technical Requirements
→ Component Characteristics

PHASE 03

Component Characteristics
→ Process Requirements

PHASE 04

Process Requirements
→ Quality Procedures

QFD scales: as few as one matrix, or as many as thirty.

04 · Step 1 — Listening

The Voice of the Customer.

Capturing the customer's needs, desires, and requirements is the most critical part of the analysis.

No single channel suffices. In practice more than one method is needed to map the full scope of what customers actually want.

Methods of Capture

Comment Cards

Rate satisfaction; flag what was loved or not.

Customer Returns

Capture the reason at the point of return.

Field Intelligence

Second-hand insight from customer-facing staff.

Focus Groups

Group dynamics surface what 1-on-1 misses.

Formal Surveys

Mass mailings — broad reach, low response rate.

Internet

Subject-oriented interest groups, online forums.

Interviews

One-on-one, in person or by telephone.

Complaint Studies

Statistical review of customer grievances.

06 · The Procedure

Six steps to a house.

  1. Identify customer requirements

    The voice of the customer — captured through cards, surveys, interviews, focus groups, and the rest. Populates Section 1.

  2. Identify product features that meet those requirements

    Translate desire into capability. Populates Section 2.

  3. Determine technical correlations among features

    The roof. Score each pair of features against each other. Populates Section 3.

  4. Build the relationship matrix

    How well does each product feature satisfy each customer requirement? Populates Section 4.

  5. Comparatively evaluate input requirements

    Score the relative importance of each customer need; benchmark competing products. Populates Section 5.

  6. Comparatively evaluate output requirements

    Score competing products against each technical feature; set targets. Populates Section 6.

08 · Why It Matters

Five things to remember.

01 · Translation, not generation

QFD doesn't invent customer requirements — it translates them into engineering language without losing fidelity.

02 · Cascading not branching

Outputs of one matrix become inputs of the next. The chain runs from customer voice all the way to shop-floor quality procedures.

03 · Numbers replace opinions

Scoring (0, 1, 3, 5) forces explicit judgment about strength of relationship and importance — and makes those judgments comparable.

04 · Competition is part of the matrix

Sections 5 and 6 fold competitive benchmarking directly into the design analysis — strengths and weaknesses become design targets.

05 · Adaptable framework, not a recipe

Use one matrix or thirty. Apply it to a product or a service. Adapt the six-section template to the problem at hand — QFD is a general framework that must be customized for context.
